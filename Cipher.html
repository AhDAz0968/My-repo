Classical Cryptography 1

Classical Cryptography refers to the traditional methods of encrypting and decrypting messages that were used before modern computer-based cryptography. These methods were primarily manual and based on simple mathematical.

Caesar Cipher

The Caesar Cipher is one of the earliest classical substitution ciphers. It was used by Julius Caesar to send secret military messages.

How it works

Each letter is shifted by a fixed number (called the key).

If the key is k, then:

E(x)=(x+k)mod26

D(x)=(x-k)mod26

where:

  • x = position of letter (A=0, B=1, ..., Z=25)
  • k = shift value
  • mod 26 = wrap around alphabet

Affine Cipher

The Affine Cipher is a more advanced classical substitution cipher.

It is based on a mathematical function of the form:

E(x)=(ax+b)mod26

Where:

  • x = position of letter (A=0, B=1, ..., Z=25)
  • a and b = keys
  • mod 26 = wrap around alphabet

How It's Different from Caesar / Shift

  • Shift Cipher: E(x)=(x+k)mod26
  • Affine Cipher: E(x)=(ax+b)mod26

Important Rule

The key a must be coprime with 26. That means gcd(a,26)=1

Decryption Formula

To decrypt, we need the modular inverse of a.

D(x)=a-1(x-b)mod26

where:

  • a-1 is the modular inverse of a modulo 26.
  • If a=5, the modular inverse of 5 mod 26 is 21 because: 5x21‚â°1(mod26)

RSA Cryptography

RSA stands for:

  • Ron Rivest
  • Adi Shamir
  • Leonard Adleman

It is a public-key cryptosystem created in 1977.

Unlike classical ciphers (Caesar, Affine, Transposition), RSA:

  • Uses two keys (public and private)
  • Is based on number theory
  • Relies on the difficulty of factoring large numbers

Type of Cipher

RSA is:

  • Asymmetric (Public-Key Cryptography)
  • Based on modular exponentiation
  • Based on prime numbers
